Overview

At a glance

This is not a single style but a long succession of image-worlds in which power, devotion, memory, and architecture are inseparable from the making of art.

Works

Selected works

Six anchors for the chapter: state image, ideal body, imperial portrait, sacred light, manuscript devotion, and the cathedral as a total work of art.

Unknown maker · c. 3100 BCE

Palette of Narmer

A ceremonial object from early dynastic Egypt, where image and political order are inseparable.

AncientEgyptRelief / ceremonial object
Why it matters

One of the clearest early statements of how rulers use images to make power feel cosmic, ordered, and inevitable.

Look for

Hierarchical scale, compressed storytelling, and symbols that matter more than optical realism.

Phidias workshop · 447–432 BCE

Parthenon sculptures

Workshop sculpture at the height of classical Athens: an art of ideal bodies, civic pride, and controlled movement.

Classical GreekAthensMarble sculpture
Why it matters

This is one of the great models later European art keeps returning to when it wants harmony, proportion, or the prestige of the classical body.

Look for

Balanced anatomy, convincing drapery, and the feeling that idealization can still preserve rhythm and life.

Unknown Roman sculptor · early 1st c. CE

Augustus of Prima Porta

Roman imperial portraiture takes Greek ideal form and bends it toward dynastic propaganda.

RomanRomeMarble sculpture
Why it matters

It shows how portraiture can present a leader as timeless, rational, and sanctioned by history even while serving immediate politics.

Look for

The mixture of individual likeness and ideal type: Augustus becomes both a man and a political image-system.

Byzantine makers · 6th–13th c.

Hagia Sophia mosaics

Byzantine art is not failing at naturalism; it is aiming somewhere else: radiance, hierarchy, and sacred presence.

ByzantineConstantinopleMosaic
Why it matters

These mosaics help reset the eye: the image here is a devotional encounter, not a window onto ordinary space.

Look for

Flattened gold space, stylized faces, and the sense that material splendor itself is part of the theology.

Insular scribes · c. 800

Book of Kells

A manuscript made by monastic scribes, where writing, ornament, and devotion collapse into one visual field.

MedievalIrelandIlluminated manuscript
Why it matters

It reminds you that medieval art is not only monumental stone or church space; it can also be intimate, portable, and almost microscopically intricate.

Look for

Pattern without empty background, disciplined symmetry, and a devotional patience that turns looking into close reading.

Context ↗

Cathedral workshops · 12th–13th c.

Chartres Cathedral

The Gothic cathedral is not a single artwork but an orchestrated image-world made by many hands across media.

GothicFranceArchitecture / stained glass
Why it matters

It expands the idea of art beyond the isolated masterpiece: building, sculpture, glass, liturgy, and bodily movement all work together.

Look for

How structure becomes feeling: height, light, and repetition are doing emotional as well as engineering work.
